The Chaos Beneath

Chaotic classical systems — like the double pendulum or billiard systems — are extremely sensitive to initial conditions. Small changes blow up exponentially and make long-term prediction impossible. Still, when we build the corresponding quantum system, surprising "scars" can appear in eigenfunctions, tracing unstable classical orbits.

Trapped Ions: Quantum Billiards

Ion traps can act like tiny billiard tables where wavefunctions live. By shaping the trap and control fields, experimentalists can tune how qubits couple to modes and engineer resilience against noise.

Quantum Scarring — Ghosts of Classical Orbits

Eric Heller first documented that some quantum eigenfunctions concentrate along unstable periodic orbits of the classical system — these are "scars." They can change how a quantum device behaves and open avenues for new control strategies.
